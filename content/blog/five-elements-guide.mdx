## Beyond the Western Four

The <GlossaryTooltip term="Wu Xing">Wu Xing</GlossaryTooltip> (<PinyinBadge pinyin="wǔ xíng" />) is often translated as the "Five Elements," but the original Chinese term conveys motion and transformation — literally "Five Phases" or "Five Movements." These are not static substances but **dynamic processes** that describe how <GlossaryTooltip term="Qi">Qi</GlossaryTooltip> manifests in the world.

The five are:

| Element | Direction | Season  | Emotion | Organ (Yin) |
| ------- | --------- | ------- | ------- | ----------- |
| Wood    | East      | Spring  | Anger   | Liver       |
| Fire    | South     | Summer  | Joy     | Heart       |
| Earth   | Center    | Late Summer | Worry | Spleen     |
| Metal   | West      | Autumn  | Grief   | Lungs       |
| Water   | North     | Winter  | Fear    | Kidneys     |

## Two Key Cycles

### The Generating Cycle (Sheng)

> Wood feeds Fire &rarr; Fire creates Earth (ash) &rarr; Earth bears Metal &rarr; Metal carries Water (condensation) &rarr; Water nourishes Wood

This is the cycle of **creation and nourishment**. Every element gives birth to the next. It is used to strengthen a weak element in a chart.

### The Controlling Cycle (Ke)

> Wood controls Earth (roots hold soil) &rarr; Earth controls Water (dams contain rivers) &rarr; Water controls Fire &rarr; Fire controls Metal (melts it) &rarr; Metal controls Wood (axe cuts tree)

This is the cycle of **restraint and balance**. No element can grow unchecked — each is held in check by another. It is used to weaken an overly dominant element.

## The <GlossaryTooltip term="Yin Yang">Yin Yang</GlossaryTooltip> Dimension

Each element has both a <GlossaryTooltip term="Yin Yang">Yin</GlossaryTooltip> and <GlossaryTooltip term="Yin Yang">Yang</GlossaryTooltip> expression. For example:

- **Yang Wood** is a tall, sturdy tree — rigid, principled, strong
- **Yin Wood** is ivy or grass — flexible, adaptable, creeping

In <GlossaryTooltip term="Ba Zi">Ba Zi</GlossaryTooltip>, the Heavenly Stems express this Yin-Yang quality for each element, adding nuance to chart interpretation.

## Why It Matters

Wu Xing thinking is everywhere in traditional Chinese culture — from medicine and martial arts to architecture and governance. Understanding it opens the door not just to Chinese astrology but to an entire worldview that sees **harmony and balance** as the foundation of well-being.
